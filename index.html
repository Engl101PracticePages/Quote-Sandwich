Creating Quote Sandwiches

A quote sandwich helps you integrate quotes smoothly, so your reader always knows what the quote is, why it is here, and how it connects to your point.

What is a quote sandwich?

A quote sandwich is a way to integrate quotes into your writing so they do not feel dropped in. Every quote gets: an introduction, the quote itself, and an explanation that connects to your point.

Why teachers care about this

Quotes do not automatically prove your point. Your job is to explain how the quote supports your claim. If you end a paragraph with a quote and no explanation, the reader has to do your thinking for you.

The three parts

Top Bread

Introduce the quote

Give context so the reader understands who is speaking and why the quote matters.

  • Name the author (usually last name).
  • Name the text or source (if needed).
  • Say what the quote is about, in your own words.
Example

In her essay “The Power of Stories,” Jones argues that storytelling is essential for human connection.

Filling

Insert the quote

Use the exact wording, and include your MLA parenthetical citation.

  • Keep quotes short unless you truly need more.
  • Use quotation marks for short quotes.
  • MLA parenthetical citation goes at the end of the sentence.
Example

Jones states, “Stories are how we make sense of the world and connect to others” (45).

Bottom Bread

Explain the quote

Interpret the quote. Do not just repeat it. Tell the reader how it supports your point.

  • What does this quote show?
  • How does it connect to your thesis or claim?
  • Why does it matter for your argument?
Example

This suggests that storytelling is not just entertainment but a fundamental way humans relate to each other.

Model quote sandwich (all together)

Notice how each sentence has a job.

Top Bread

In her essay “The Power of Stories,” Jones argues that storytelling is essential for human connection.

Filling

Jones states, “Stories are how we make sense of the world and connect to others” (45).

Bottom Bread

This suggests that storytelling is not just a form of entertainment but a fundamental way humans relate to each other.

Build your own quote sandwich (Occupational Outlook Handbook)

What you do

  1. Open the Occupational Outlook Handbook page for your chosen career.
  2. Find one short quote (1 to 2 sentences) that gives a useful fact or claim about the career.
  3. Write a quote sandwich using that quote.
  4. Submit it on Canvas (copy and paste).
What counts as a good quote for this
  • Job duties, work environment, pay, growth outlook, education, skills, or licensing.
  • Something that helps you explain what the career is really like.
  • Something you can respond to and interpret (not just a random sentence).

Rules

  • Quote must be 1 to 2 sentences max.
  • Your Bottom Bread must be at least 2 sentences.
  • Your explanation must connect to your career goal (why it matters for your plan).
  • If the page does not show page numbers, that is fine. Do not invent a page number.

Tip: Aim for a quote you can actually analyze, not just one you can copy.

Copy and paste template

Fill this in. Keep the language simple. Your Bottom Bread should explain the meaning and why it matters for your plan. 

Top Bread (Introduce):
On the Occupational Outlook Handbook page for [Career], the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics explains [what the quote is about].

Filling (Quote + MLA parenthetical if available):
The OOH states, “[paste quote here]”.

Bottom Bread (Explain):
This matters because [your interpretation that does not just repeat the info from the quote]. Specifically, [how it affects the choices of a student choosing this career path: major, training, skills, expectations, etc.].

Works Cited line (copy and paste)

Replace the brackets. For OOH pages, you will almost always use the web citation format and include an access date. 

“[Page Title].” Occupational Outlook Handbook, U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, [URL]. Accessed [Month Day, Year].

Canvas submission

Submit to the assignment: Quote Sandwich (OOH)

What to paste into Canvas

  1. Your full 3-part quote sandwich (Top Bread, Filling, Bottom Bread).
  2. Your Works Cited line.

If you want to format it cleanly in Canvas, add line breaks between parts. If Canvas removes your spacing, that is fine.

Quick checklist

Did I introduce the quote (who, what, why this quote is here)?

Did I keep the quote short and copy it exactly?

Did I include MLA citation info when available (and not invent page numbers)?

Did I explain what the quote shows and how it supports my point?

Did I write at least 2 sentences of Bottom Bread (analysis, not summary)?

Did I avoid ending my paragraph with a quote?

Common issues
  • Dropping a quote with no Top Bread.
  • Explaining what the quote says instead of what it does (analysis).
  • Using a quote that is so long that your explanation becomes tiny.
  • Using a quote that is only a definition or a bland statement you cannot interpret.
